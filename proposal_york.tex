\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
% ============================================================

The rapid global adoption of battery electric vehicles (BEVs) is
fundamentally reshaping urban transportation systems, creating urgent
demand for strategically planned fast-charging infrastructure.  Despite
considerable scholarly and practitioner interest in optimizing the
location of public charging stations, the methods and frameworks
currently available to urban planners, transportation agencies, and
utilities remain fragmented, technically incomplete, and poorly
integrated across planning scales.  This dissertation addresses the
methodological gaps that prevent the effective, equitable, and
operationally coherent deployment of urban BEV fast-charging networks.

A PRISMA~2020-compliant systematic review of 200 peer-reviewed studies
published between 2011 and 2026 reveals five persistent and interrelated
methodological gaps.  First, administrative boundaries --- traffic
analysis zones, census tracts, and municipal districts --- systematically
misalign with actual mobility corridor patterns, introducing spatial bias
into demand estimation and station placement (133 papers provide partial
evidence).  Second, despite zoning regulations determining the legal
permissibility of charging infrastructure, no study systematically
compares planning outcomes under alternative zoning schemas (only
5~papers).  Third, equity of geographic access and infrastructure
utilization efficiency are treated as competing single objectives rather
than jointly optimized through a unified model (only 14 papers address
both).  Fourth, static single-period optimization dominates over
adaptive, budget-constrained phased deployment with explicit
phase-transition trigger criteria (44 papers partially address phasing).
Fifth, no validated framework bridges city-scale rollout planning to
individual site implementation specifications (11 papers).

The proposed dissertation develops and validates a three-stage integrated
research framework, grounded in a completed preliminary study of
mobility-corridor zone construction applied to Toronto, Canada.
Stage~A constructs mobility-corridor spatial units from origin-destination
data and conducts the first systematic comparative analysis of alternative
zoning schemas.  Stage~B formulates a joint equity-utilization
bi-objective optimization model with Pareto frontier characterization
and extends it to multi-stage adaptive phased deployment.  Stage~C
develops and validates a meso-to-micro site translation protocol
specifying the minimum information set required to operationalize
corridor-level rollout plans at the individual site level.

Five methodological contributions are expected, each directly resolving
one identified gap: a spatial alignment score metric; a zoning schema
comparison procedure; a joint equity-utilization Pareto optimization
model; an adaptive phase-transition decision procedure; and a
meso-to-micro translation protocol.  Together these outputs provide
a transferable, evidence-grounded planning framework applicable to any
metropolitan area with available origin-destination, demographic, and
land-use data.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} BEV fast-charging infrastructure;
urban spatial optimization; equity and utilization planning;
phased deployment; meso-micro integration

\section{Purpose and Research Goals}
% ============================================================

Cities worldwide are accelerating the transition to electric vehicles as
a cornerstone of their climate and air-quality strategies.  As BEV
ownership grows, the availability of reliable, well-placed fast-charging
stations becomes a critical determinant of whether this transition is
equitable, efficient, and sustained.  Yet city planners, transportation
agencies, and utilities currently lack a coherent planning methodology
that simultaneously addresses where stations should go, when they should
be built relative to evolving demand, who should be served first, and how
a city-level deployment plan is translated into individual site decisions.

The purpose of this dissertation is to develop and validate an integrated
research framework that closes the principal methodological gaps in urban
BEV fast-charging infrastructure planning.  The research is motivated by
three practical observations.  First, most existing planning approaches
place charging stations within administrative districts (e.g.\ census
tracts, traffic analysis zones) that bear little relation to how people
actually drive and charge their vehicles.  The result is systematic
mismatch between station locations and real-world demand.  Second, equity
and efficiency are routinely treated as competing goals, leaving planners
without the evidence base to make defensible trade-off decisions.  Third,
planning is overwhelmingly static: a network is optimized for a single
point in time, with no mechanism for adapting deployment as adoption
evolves.  Each of these shortcomings compounds the others, and none has
been resolved in the existing literature.

The proposed dissertation directly addresses these limitations through
four research questions spanning spatial unit design, joint equity and
utilization optimization, adaptive phased rollout, and the operational
translation from urban-scale plans to individual site specifications.  The
work is grounded in a PRISMA-compliant systematic review of 200 papers
published between 2011 and 2026, and it draws on a completed preliminary
study of mobility-corridor zone construction applied to the Toronto
metropolitan area, which provides empirical proof of concept for the
spatial methodology proposed in Stage~A.

\section{Systematic Review Methodology}
% ============================================================

This review follows the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews
and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA 2020) statement \citep{page2021}.  Primary
searches were conducted on arXiv (categories cs.AI, cs.SY, eess.SY,
math.OC, econ.GN) supplemented by Semantic Scholar and Web of Science,
using query clusters targeting the five identified gap themes: spatial
unit alignment, zoning impact, equity-utilization joint optimization,
phased deployment, and meso-micro integration.  After deduplication by
arXiv ID and title-year matching, 380 records were screened; 153 full
texts were assessed for eligibility; 200 studies were included.
Inclusion required a spatial or planning dimension, a reproducible
methodology, English language, and publication between 2011 and 2026.
For each included paper, structured data extraction was performed across
13 dimensions covering methodology, spatial unit, equity treatment,
utilization modeling, rollout strategy, and gap mapping.

\subsection{Overview and Thematic Organization}

The 200 included studies are organized into six thematic clusters based
on their primary methodological and substantive contribution
(Table~3.1).  Spatial optimization for charging station placement is the
dominant cluster (133 papers, 66.5\%), reflecting the field's
entrenchment in facility-location modelling as the primary analytical
lens.  The remaining clusters --- utilization modelling and demand
forecasting (16 papers), equity planning and environmental justice
(6 papers), phased and sequential deployment (7 papers), meso--micro
scale integration (2 papers), and review and synthesis (3 papers) ---
are substantially smaller, pointing to the structural imbalances that
motivate the five research gaps.  Eighteen papers span two or more
clusters and are catalogued under their primary contribution.

\subsection{Spatial Optimization for Charging Station Placement}

\textit{133 papers --- 66.5\% of the reviewed corpus.}

Spatial optimization constitutes the dominant methodological paradigm in
charging infrastructure planning research.  These studies apply
deterministic and stochastic mathematical programming models, principally
mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), bilevel formulations,
flow-based facility location, and, more recently, deep reinforcement
learning, to identify optimal station locations subject to demand
coverage, budget, and network constraints.  The cluster spans four
decades of methodological development, from early grid-placement
heuristics to modern quantum-computing formulations, and encompasses a
wide range of spatial scales from individual sites to regional multi-city
networks.  Despite this sophistication, the spatial unit of analysis
remains almost universally fixed to administrative boundaries, and
single-period static optimization remains the norm.

\paragraph{MILP and bilevel formulations.}
The most established stream applies MILP to minimize weighted demand
distance or maximize coverage under budgetary and capacity constraints.
\citet{nankali2025} derive the first exact solution algorithm for
large-scale EVCS placement via a bilevel programme in which the
upper-level planner selects station locations to maximize net revenue
while lower-level users choose routes through a battery-expanded network
that explicitly encodes range anxiety.  \citet{okada2025} reformulate
joint station placement and EV routing as a quadratic unconstrained
binary optimization (QUBO) problem solvable by quantum annealers,
demonstrating significant speed advantages over classical branch-and-bound
for moderate-scale instances.  \citet{choi2025} combine a genetic
algorithm with a fuzzy analytic hierarchy process to optimize both
location and capacity of stations, capturing multi-criteria preferences
that cannot be reduced to a single weighted objective.  Earlier
foundational contributions include \citet{brandstatter2020}, who solve
the location problem for car-sharing fleets with range constraints, and
\citet{anjos2020}, who study fast CS deployment to maximize EV adoption
with coverage and budget constraints.

\paragraph{Mobile and joint fixed-mobile CS planning.}
Several recent studies extend classical placement models to include mobile
charging stations (MCSs) as flexible supply resources.  \citet{najafzad2026}
formulate a two-stage stochastic MILP for simultaneous deployment of
fixed and mobile stations across multiple zones and periods, explicitly
modelling charging demand uncertainty through scenario trees.
\citet{yu2025} address joint planning of fixed and mobile stations using
an improved alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) algorithm,
demonstrating that coordinated FCS--MCS deployment reduces total system
cost and improves spatial coverage relative to independent planning.
\citet{zheng2025} integrate investment decisions with charging
assignments to capture spatial--temporal demand dynamics and their
interdependencies across the planning horizon.  \citet{ai2025} develop a
location-inventory-grid model using continuous approximation to overcome
the intractability of discrete formulations for joint station location,
battery inventory, and grid interaction optimization.

\paragraph{Data-driven and machine-learning approaches.}
Since 2023, data-driven approaches have gained substantial traction as
large-scale charging transaction datasets have become available.
\citet{huang2025} introduce a spatially aware machine learning (SAML)
method that combines a multi-layer perceptron with a spatial loss
function to capture nonlinear spatial dependencies between urban activity
patterns and optimal EVCS locations --- a dependency that traditional
statistical models cannot represent because they ignore geographic
autocorrelation.  \citet{silva2025} use a quasi-experimental design
with mobile phone GPS data and EVCS deployment history to measure the
causal impact of station placement on local business visitation patterns,
providing one of the few empirical validations of demand spillover
effects.  \citet{alharbi2025} propose a spatial--temporal flow-driven
(STFD) model with uncertainty quantification for data-driven CS
infrastructure planning under demand variability.  \citet{islam2025}
present Charge-Map, a data-driven simulation-optimisation framework
that plans infrastructure expansion given existing station locations and
current demand observations, explicitly modelling the spatial
heterogeneity of individual EV owner charging behaviour.

\paragraph{Joint routing and placement.}
Operational decisions interact with strategic siting in formulations
that jointly optimize station location and EV routing.
\citet{nakao2025} address fleet-size and charging infrastructure planning
for demand-responsive feeder services under stochastic demand and
user-defined CO$_2$ targets.  \citet{zhong2025} integrate land-use and
transportation modelling for electric bus CS placement, linking station
siting to scheduled routes and depot constraints.  \citet{bertucci2026}
jointly optimize charging infrastructure design and the operational
schedule of electric ferries, demonstrating significant cost reductions
relative to sequential planning.

\paragraph{Geographic and regulatory contexts.}
The spatial optimization literature spans a wide range of geographic
contexts, though studies in North America and China dominate.
\citet{fariza2026} apply analytic hierarchy process (AHP) and GIS
to plan public EVCS in West Java, Indonesia, explicitly accounting for
spatial imbalance in infrastructure readiness relative to rapid EV
adoption growth.  \citet{karakus2025} develop RSERI-EV, a spatially
explicit multi-modal risk assessment framework integrating flood risk,
land-surface temperature extremes, vegetation indices, and road
accessibility to generate a composite resilience score for EVCS
vulnerability assessment.  \citet{do2025} develop a digital twin
framework applied to a localized urban site in Hanoi, Vietnam, combining
agent-based simulation with embedded optimization to evaluate station
configurations and renewable energy integration under policy scenarios.

\subsection{Utilization Modelling and Demand Forecasting}

\textit{16 papers --- 8.0\% of the reviewed corpus.}

Utilization modelling addresses the frequency, duration, and intensity
with which deployed stations are used, and how utilization responds to
placement, pricing, and adoption dynamics.  The central challenge is
endogeneity: utilization is partly determined by siting (a well-located
station generates demand through convenience effects), yet siting
optimization requires utilization forecasts as inputs.  None of the
reviewed papers fully resolves this circularity.

\citet{nguyen2025} analyse three finite queueing systems
(M/M/1/K, M/M/$s$/K, and M/E$_r$/$s$/K) in competitive charging markets
with multiple service providers, deriving closed-form expressions for
wait times, abandonment rates, and occupancy under varying EV penetration
levels.  Their results demonstrate that ignoring competitive queueing
dynamics substantially overestimates station utilization in multi-operator
markets.  \citet{junker2025} apply causal discovery techniques to
337,344 charging events across 100 stations in Palo Alto and Boulder,
California, revealing latent causal structure between station
characteristics and observed utilization.  Proximity to daily activity
destinations (workplace, grocery, retail) emerges as the dominant causal
driver of utilization, ahead of pricing and connector type --- a finding
with direct implications for demand-side siting criteria.

Demand forecasting provides the supply-side inputs for utilization
modelling.  \citet{huttel2021} combine deep spatio-temporal models with
optimization to forecast charging demand at candidate sites, enabling
forward-looking placement decisions that account for adoption trajectory
rather than current snapshot demand.  \citet{gopalakrishnan2016} develop
multi-step demand prediction models for charging infrastructure sizing,
demonstrating that naive linear extrapolation of current adoption
consistently overestimates long-run demand at saturation and
underestimates short-run demand at inflection.  \citet{al_dahabreh2023}
propose a data-driven framework for improving public EVCS infrastructure
using historical spatio-temporal charging records, showing that
behavioural adaptation (schedule shifting, destination switching) reduces
effective demand growth rates in high-adoption scenarios.

\subsection{Equity Planning and Environmental Justice}

\textit{6 papers primary; 14 papers with partial evidence.}

Equity in EV charging infrastructure refers to the equitable distribution
of charging access across socioeconomic groups, geographic areas, and
transportation need profiles.  Low-income communities, multi-family
housing residents, and residents in underserved corridors face a compound
disadvantage: lower rates of private parking (limiting home charging),
lower proximity to commercial charging networks, and higher exposure to
vehicle emissions from conventional transportation.

\citet{babur2026} develop a regional transportation model for equitable
EVCS design, demonstrating that cross-jurisdictional mobility patterns
create systematic coverage gaps for low-income communities
disproportionately affected by traffic-related air pollution.  This gap
is only detectable at regional scale: municipal-boundary optimization
masks it by construction.  \citet{jha2025} formulate an equity-based
MILP incorporating Gini coefficient and coverage ratio as formal fairness
objectives for EVCS placement in underserved communities, providing the
most rigorous single-study treatment of equity-constrained siting in the
corpus.  \citet{yuan2025_fairness} propose fairness-oriented CS location
optimization driven by deep reinforcement learning, demonstrating that
DRL-based multi-objective formulations achieve better equity-efficiency
frontiers than classical single-objective MILP in high-dimensional state
spaces.  \citet{erfani2024} examine crowdfunding mechanisms for
neighbourhood-scale charging infrastructure, finding that community-based
financing substantially improves equity of access in areas underserved
by commercial deployment incentives.  \citet{arief2023} develop a robust
optimization model for developing-country urban contexts under electricity
supply uncertainty, explicitly treating equity of spatial coverage as a
hard constraint rather than a secondary consideration.  \citet{khan2021}
document the structural disadvantage of multi-family housing residents
who lack home charging access and model the public fast-charging
infrastructure required to compensate.

Despite this body of work, equity metrics vary widely across studies:
Gini coefficients, demographic coverage ratios, accessibility-weighted
distances, and qualitative narrative assessments are all used, preventing
cumulative comparison.  No study provides both a Pareto frontier and a
policy-interpretable equity threshold, the two elements planners require
to make defensible equity-efficiency trade-off decisions.

\subsection{Phased and Sequential Deployment}

\textit{7 papers primary; 44 papers with partial evidence.}

Real-world charging infrastructure deployment proceeds in phases:
capital is allocated in budget cycles, demand evolves with adoption, and
early siting decisions constrain later options through regulatory
commitments and network externalities.  Despite this, the dominant
modelling paradigm treats deployment as a single-period event.  The
44~papers providing partial phasing evidence predominantly model
pre-defined temporal stages rather than adaptive, demand-responsive
sequences.

\citet{najafzad2026} and \citet{yu2025} both incorporate multi-period
planning, defining phases exogenously (Year 1, Year 2, Year 3) rather
than through trigger criteria derived from observed performance.
\citet{tang2025} develop a stochastic V2G charging station deployment
model using scenario trees to represent demand uncertainty, but define
phase transitions by calendar rather than by utilization threshold.
\citet{shen2024} propose sequential charging station planning that
updates siting decisions as adoption data accumulates across periods,
representing the closest approximation to adaptive phasing in the corpus
while still relying on fixed temporal checkpoints rather than
performance-triggered transitions.  \citet{yuan2025b} apply a
demand-driven multi-objective model to Chongqing, differentiating
deployment across BEV, EREV, and PHEV vehicle types over successive
planning periods.

\citet{guillet2020} and \citet{anjos2020} study the sequencing of fast
CS rollout to maximize adoption coverage under budget constraints,
providing foundational results on the value of sequential over
simultaneous deployment.  The key unresolved problem across all 44
papers is the definition of phase-transition trigger criteria: when
observed utilization, coverage shortfall, or adoption rate exceeds a
threshold, what operational decision rule determines that expansion to
the next phase is warranted?  This question is entirely absent from the
reviewed literature.

\subsection{Meso--Micro Scale Integration}

\textit{2 papers primary; 11 papers with partial evidence.}

Only 11 of 200 papers engage with the meso--micro planning scale
integration problem, and none provides a validated translation protocol.
The gap exists at the boundary between transportation planning
(city or corridor scale) and urban design and site engineering (parcel
and street scale).  Planning practice implicitly performs this
translation through professional judgement, but without a validated,
transferable protocol the quality and consistency of translation varies
widely across practitioners and contexts.

\citet{do2025} develop a digital twin framework applied to a university
campus in Hanoi, Vietnam, combining agent-based simulation with embedded
optimisation to evaluate operational policies, station configurations,
and renewable energy integration at the site level.  The methodology
demonstrates high-fidelity micro-scale modelling but does not specify
how a city-level rollout plan would determine the study-area boundary or
the candidate-site set.  \citet{ai2025} bridge some meso (grid-capacity
and location) and micro (battery inventory) dimensions through a
location-inventory-grid model, but do not address land-use compatibility,
pedestrian access geometry, or the information transfer from corridor
allocation to individual site scoring.  \citet{erfani2024} operate at
neighbourhood scale, examining financing mechanisms for local
infrastructure, implicitly bridging district-level demand allocation and
individual site viability.  \citet{jin2022} develop an optimization
framework for EVCS development that incorporates both corridor-scale
demand allocation and individual station sizing.  None of these studies
specifies the minimum information set that must flow from meso rollout
plan to micro site decision, defines a site suitability scoring function
derived from meso outputs, or provides a feedback procedure for
reconciling micro-level infeasibilities with meso-level allocations.

\subsection{Review and Synthesis Studies}

\textit{3 papers --- 1.5\% of the reviewed corpus.}

Three included papers provide meta-level synthesis of the broader
charging infrastructure planning field.  \citet{varma2023} conduct a
systematic review of EV adoption barriers, benefits, and solutions,
synthesizing consumer survey evidence across multiple countries and
identifying charging infrastructure availability as the dominant stated
barrier to adoption --- ahead of purchase price, range anxiety, and grid
reliability in most market contexts.  \citet{munawar2024} provide a
narrative review of EV integration in urban logistics using GIS-based
multi-case analysis across metropolitan areas, examining efficiency,
environmental impact, and policy dimensions of freight electrification.
\citet{alhussan2024} present a comparative review of nature-inspired
metaheuristics for EVCS placement optimization, providing taxonomic
coverage of the MILP-vs-metaheuristic methodological divide and
demonstrating that Graylag Goose Optimizer achieves competitive solution
quality at lower computational cost than genetic algorithms and PSO on
benchmark placement instances.  Collectively, the three review papers
confirm the dominant focus on single-period spatial optimization and the
relative neglect of equity, phasing, and meso--micro integration, providing
external validation of the five-gap framework.

\section{Gap Analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Gap 1: Misaligned Spatial Units (133 papers with partial evidence)}

The spatial unit of analysis is almost universally fixed to
administrative boundaries --- traffic analysis zones, census tracts, or
municipal districts --- that poorly represent actual BEV mobility
patterns.  Trip-based corridor flows routinely cross these boundaries,
producing systematic bias in demand estimation and station placement
\citep{babur2026,do2025}.  No study in the corpus constructs
mobility-corridor-based spatial units and directly compares siting
outcomes against a TAZ-based baseline on the same metropolitan area.

\subsection{Gap 2: Absence of Zoning Impact Analysis (5 papers)}

Zoning regulations determine legally permissible charging station
locations, yet no study systematically compares planning outcomes under
alternative zoning schemas.  The five papers touching this dimension
treat zoning as a binary constraint rather than an analytical variable
\citep{karakus2025,fariza2026}.  This is the most severe methodological
absence relative to its policy importance.

\subsection{Gap 3: Equity and Utilization Treated Separately (14 papers address both)}

Of 200 reviewed papers, only 14 measure both equity and utilization, and
none jointly optimizes both within a single multi-objective formulation
yielding a quantified trade-off frontier \citep{arief2023,najafzad2026}.
This separation leaves planners without the evidence base needed for
defensible deployment decisions under competing objectives.

\subsection{Gap 4: Static Optimization Dominance (44 papers address phasing)}

The vast majority of models optimize a single planning period.
Budget-constrained, adaptive deployment with explicit phase-transition
trigger criteria is rare \citep{zhang_tan2024,tang2025}.  Real-world
deployment is subject to budget cycles, demand uncertainty, and
infrastructure irreversibility --- conditions where adaptive sequential
decision-making is theoretically superior, but this advantage has not
been quantified.

\subsection{Gap 5: Missing Meso-to-Micro Integration (11 papers)}

City-level rollout plans cannot be operationalized at the site level
without an explicit translation protocol specifying the information that
must flow from corridor-scale allocation to individual site selection.
No such protocol exists in the reviewed literature \citep{do2025},
leaving site selection to proceed ad hoc and potentially violating the
spatial intent of the rollout plan.

\section{Research Questions}
% ============================================================

The five gaps motivate four research questions.  Each question is
designed to produce a measurable methodological output while collectively
constituting an integrated research program.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1em,labelindent=0em]

\item[\textbf{RQ1} (Gaps 1 \& 2):] How can mobility-corridor-aligned
  spatial units and comparative zoning schema analysis be integrated into
  urban BEV charging station siting to reduce spatial demand misalignment
  and quantify the planning consequences of zoning schema choice?

\item[\textbf{RQ2} (Gap 3):] How can equity of geographic access and
  infrastructure utilization efficiency be simultaneously optimized in
  charging network design, and what are the Pareto-efficient trade-offs
  between these objectives that planners can use to inform deployment
  decisions?

\item[\textbf{RQ3} (Gap 4):] What sequencing criteria and adaptive
  decision rules enable effective, budget-constrained phased deployment
  of BEV fast-charging infrastructure under demand uncertainty, and how
  much value does adaptive phasing create relative to static single-period
  optimization?

\item[\textbf{RQ4} (Gap 5):] How can meso-level rollout plans be
  systematically translated to micro-level site implementation
  specifications, and what minimum information set must flow from the
  meso to the micro planning scale to ensure technical and planning
  feasibility?

\end{description}

\section{Proposed Research Framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Overview}

The research framework comprises three sequential stages.  Each stage
produces validated outputs that serve as direct inputs to the next,
creating a vertically integrated planning methodology from city-scale
spatial analysis to site-level implementation.

\subsection{Stage A: Mobility-Corridor Spatial Unit Construction and
  Zoning Schema Comparison (RQ1)}

Stage A constructs BEV-mobility-aligned spatial zones as an alternative
to administrative boundaries.  Major arterial road segments are extracted
from a full road centreline network using GIS processing, and the
resulting arterial graph is partitioned into contiguous zones that reflect
actual travel corridors.  BEV registration counts are proportionally
redistributed to these zones using areal intersection weights, producing
a corridor-aligned demand field.  Ridge-path extraction identifies
sequences of zones with locally elevated BEV density, delineating the
structural backbone of BEV activity.  The existing fast-charging network
is then benchmarked against the corridor structure using a graph-theoretic
Weighted Proximity Distance metric, tested against a null distribution of
randomized configurations.

A preliminary study applying this procedure to the Toronto metropolitan
area yielded 234 arterial zones from over 40,000 road segments and found
that 85.7\% of randomized configurations achieved better spatial alignment
than the actual deployed network, establishing the empirical motivation
for corridor-aligned planning.  Stage A further conducts a zoning schema
comparison, evaluating siting outcomes under the current municipal
ordinance alongside at least two alternative permitting frameworks, and
quantifies the equity, utilization, and cost consequences of each schema
choice.

\subsection{Stage B: Joint Equity-Utilization Adaptive Phased Rollout
  Optimization (RQ2, RQ3)}

Stage B formulates a bi-objective mixed-integer optimization model using
the corridor zones from Stage A as the spatial frame.  The two objectives
are: (i)~equity, measured as the Gini coefficient of spatial
accessibility across corridor zones weighted by population; and
(ii)~utilization, measured as mean station fill rate across the planning
horizon.  The $\varepsilon$-constraint method is used to map the full
Pareto frontier, providing planners with a quantified equity-utilization
trade-off curve.

The multi-period extension introduces phase-transition trigger criteria:
when mean utilization in deployed stations exceeds a threshold, the model
activates the next deployment phase.  Monte Carlo demand scenarios
represent adoption uncertainty.  The adaptive model is compared against a
static single-period benchmark to quantify the value of adaptive phasing.

\subsection{Stage C: Meso-to-Micro Site Translation Protocol (RQ4)}

Stage C operationalizes the Stage B rollout schedule by defining the
information transfer from corridor-level allocation decisions to
individual site selection.  The translation protocol specifies: (a)~the
minimum viable information set flowing from meso to micro (zone
identifier, deployment phase, charging tier, coverage radius, equity
priority flag); (b)~a site suitability scoring function incorporating
land-use compatibility, grid connection proximity, pedestrian
accessibility, and parking geometry; and (c)~a conflict resolution
procedure for micro-level infeasibilities.  The protocol is validated
against documented site deployment decisions in at least one metropolitan
context, using a consistency score measuring alignment between
protocol-ranked sites and actual selections.

\subsection{Data Requirements and Study Area}

The framework is applicable to any metropolitan area providing:
origin-destination trip or mobile-device mobility data; municipal zoning
GIS layers; observed charging demand data; census-tract demographic data;
grid infrastructure maps; and a documented city charging rollout plan.
The Toronto metropolitan area provides the Stage A proof-of-concept
dataset; one additional metropolitan context will be used for Stage C
validation.

\subsection{Dissertation Chapter Outline}

The dissertation is organized into six chapters.  Each chapter
corresponds to a distinct stage of the research program, from
problem framing and literature synthesis through empirical framework
development to integration and conclusion.

\textbf{Chapter 1 --- Introduction and Motivation.}
Introduces the urban BEV fast-charging infrastructure problem,
situates it within the policy landscape of global electrification
mandates, and establishes the five-gap analytical framework that
organizes the dissertation.  Key terms --- spatial unit, equity,
utilization, phased deployment, and meso-micro integration ---
are defined precisely for use throughout.

\textbf{Chapter 2 --- Systematic Literature Review.}
Presents the PRISMA~2020 review protocol, the 200-paper corpus, and
the thematic analysis across six literature clusters.  Documents
the evidence base for all five gaps through structured extraction
tables and gap-coverage analysis.  This chapter constitutes the
primary body of evidence for the research questions.

\textbf{Chapter 3 --- Stage A: Mobility-Corridor Spatial Unit
Construction and Zoning Schema Comparison.}
Develops the arterial-corridor zone methodology from GIS road
centreline and origin-destination data.  Benchmarks corridor-based
zones against TAZ and FSA baselines using the Weighted Proximity
Distance metric.  Conducts the first systematic comparative analysis
of alternative zoning schemas for a metropolitan charging network.
Builds on the completed Toronto proof-of-concept study.

\textbf{Chapter 4 --- Stage B: Joint Equity-Utilization Adaptive
Phased Rollout Optimization.}
Formulates the bi-objective mixed-integer optimization model with
Gini-coefficient equity and mean utilization objectives.  Computes
the Pareto frontier using the $\varepsilon$-constraint method.
Extends the model to multi-stage stochastic programming with
adaptive phase-transition trigger criteria and validates it against
static single-period benchmarks under demand uncertainty.

\textbf{Chapter 5 --- Stage C: Meso-to-Micro Site Translation Protocol.}
Defines the translation protocol specifying the minimum information
set from corridor allocation to site decision.  Develops the site
suitability scoring function incorporating land-use compatibility,
grid proximity, pedestrian access, and parking geometry.  Validates
the protocol against documented site deployment decisions in at
least one metropolitan context.

\textbf{Chapter 6 --- Conclusions and Contributions.}
Synthesizes findings across all three stages, articulates the five
methodological contributions, discusses generalizability and
limitations, and outlines directions for future research.

\section{Expected Contributions and Timeline}

\subsection{Expected Contributions}

This dissertation produces five primary contributions, each directly
resolving one of the identified research gaps:

\textbf{Contribution 1, Mobility-Corridor Spatial Unit Methodology
(Gap~1).}
A validated method for constructing mobility-corridor spatial zones from
origin-destination data, with a quantified spatial unit alignment score
and demonstrated reduction in spatial demand estimation error relative to
administrative TAZ baselines.  This is the first methodology in the
literature that directly questions and empirically tests the validity of
the spatial frame for charging infrastructure planning.

\textbf{Contribution 2, Zoning Schema Comparison Procedure (Gap~2).}
The first systematic comparative analysis of alternative zoning schemas
for charging infrastructure siting, producing a replicable schema
comparison procedure that quantifies the coverage, equity, cost, and
utilization consequences of zoning schema design choices.  This
contribution provides evidence that has been entirely absent from the
planning literature.

\textbf{Contribution 3, Joint Equity-Utilization Optimization Model
(Gap~3).}
A bi-objective optimization model with formal Pareto frontier
characterization that simultaneously optimizes equity of spatial access
and infrastructure utilization efficiency.  The Pareto frontier provides
planners with quantified trade-off information, translating a
political/ethical question (how much equity is worth how much efficiency?)
into a technically grounded, reproducible analysis.

\textbf{Contribution 4, Adaptive Phasing Decision Procedure (Gap~4).}
A multi-stage stochastic programming model with adaptive phase-transition
trigger criteria, validated against demand uncertainty scenarios, with
quantified comparison to static single-period optimization.  The decision
procedure is operationally specified, defining when (trigger criteria) and
how (reallocation rules) to advance from one deployment phase to the next.

\textbf{Contribution 5, Meso-to-Micro Translation Protocol (Gap~5).}
A formalized information translation protocol bridging meso-level rollout
planning to micro-level site selection, with a validated site suitability
scoring function and consistency score against real-world deployment
decisions.  This contribution closes the operational gap between planning
and implementation that currently prevents rollout plans from being
systematically operationalized.
